Corruption in Kenya

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Corruption in Kenya has historically been a major societal and economic challenge, impacting public administration, economic development, and international investment. According to global monitors like Transparency International, the vice spans both minor street-level bribery and multi-billion shilling institutional scandals[1].

Historical Content [edit]

Since independence, Kenya has experienced several high-profile corruption scandals that have shaped its political landscape. Major historical events, such as the Goldenberg scandal in the 1990s and the Anglo-Leasing affair in the early 2000s, involved billions of shillings diverted from the public treasury through fraudulent government procurement and fake contracts[2].

Highly Affected Sectors [edit]

While governance frameworks have continuously evolved, several key sectors have consistently drawn scrutiny from internal and external oversight bodies[3]:

  • Public Procurement: Inflation of prices for goods and services purchased by national and county governments.
  • Traffic Enforcement: Petty bribery involving public transport operators (such as matatus) and traffic police officers[1].
  • Land Administration: Fake title deeds, illegal allocations of public public utility lands, and bureaucratic extortion at land registries.

Anti-Corruption Efforts[ edit]

Kenya has established several legal institutions to combat the vice, most notably the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (EACC) and the Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions (ODPP)[3]. Additionally, the adoption of digital systems—such as the e-Citizen portal and the Integrated Financial Management Information System (IFMIS)—aims to minimize physical human interaction and increase financial accountability[2].
